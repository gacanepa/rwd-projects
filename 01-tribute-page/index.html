Guido van Rossum

Creator of the Python programming language

Key moments in Guido's life:

  • 1956 - Born in Haarlem, Netherlands
  • 1982 - Receives a master's degree in mathematics and computer science from the University of Amsterdam
  • Early 1990s - Creates Python
  • 1995 - Moves to the US
  • 1999 - Submits a funding proposal to DARPA called Computer Programming for Everybody with an outline of his goals for Python:
    • An easy and intuitive language just as powerful as major competitors
    • Open source, so anyone can contribute to its development
    • Code that is as understandable as plain English
    • Suitability for everyday tasks, allowing for short development times
  • 2000-2003 - Works at Zope Corporation
  • 2005-2012 - Works at Google, spending half of his time developing Python
  • 2013 until present - Works at Dropbox

Typically when you ask a programmer to explain to a lay person what a programming language is, they will say that it is how you tell a computer what to do. But if that was all, why would they be so passionate about programming languages when they talk among themselves?

In reality, programming languages are how programmers express and communicate ideas — and the audience for those ideas is other programmers, not computers. The reason: the computer can take care of itself, but programmers are always working with other programmers, and poorly communicated ideas can cause expensive flops. In fact, ideas expressed in a programming language also often reach the end users of the program — people who will never read or even know about the program, but who nevertheless are affected by it.

Think of the incredible success of companies like Google or Facebook. At the core of these are ideas — ideas about what computers can do for people. To be effective, an idea must be expressed as a computer program, using a programming language. The language that is best to express an idea will give the team using that language a key advantage, because it gives the team members — people! — clarity about that idea.
